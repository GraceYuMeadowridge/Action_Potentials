How to manage your time?

Personally, I have been one of the more fortunate ones with time management. I often seem to be able to avoid procrastination and almost always turn in assignments early. However, it was not always easy for me.

At my old school, the transition to 6th grade took a toll on me. My friends and I used to have an infinite amount of free time suddenly find ourselves in a heavy workload. We started to find ourselves stuck with project works that we neglected until the last few days before submission.

A friend and I decided that we need to change this situation - we made a deal to finish every assignment in the first half of the allocated time. Or else the other person has unalienated rights to come to each other's house and "kindly remind" there is a task due soon. For example, if the assignment is due a week after it was assigned, we must finish it three days before the due date. If not, you will be harassed until you finish the task. Since then, I have never violated that pact, although I might have had to cooperate with his sister to drag him away from his fascinating experiment with bacteria growth to work on his Language Art project. Sometimes I still feel guilty that all his bacteria in that experiment died because we did not refrigerate them soon enough. Nonetheless, we saved him from staying up to 11pm working on the project.

From then, I started to manage my time to the minute, strictly sticking to a schedule. I eventually grow reliant on it. Nowadays, without any conscious effort, I will get up in the morning to plan my day. A few years later, while I watched a self-improvement coach talking about time management, I learned the what I did was leveraging myself.

The definition of leverage is a mechanical advantage. It can also mean power or effectiveness. Time is powerful; it is the only key resource that humans have yet to figure how to neither trade nor regenerate. Time is your constraint to achieve your potential. You will not be able to negotiate with it. Therefore, make the best of it, leverage yourself to spend time more efficiently.

Another issue with time seems to deal with scaling. I know a few perfectionists who would edit their art or PE reflection like an English essay, with immaculate grammar and punctuation. There is nothing wrong with doing so until you realize that they spend the better part of their weekend trying to make it perfect. In the end, your PE and art teacher will not mind any tiny grammatical error because they are solely looking for its contents.

When I talk about scaling, I am addressing how reward and time correlate. There are three types of scaling - linear, sublinear, and threshold-dependent. A linear reward scale means the more you do, the better you get, much like sports. A sublinear reward scale means that you will get better as you practice, but only until a point. For example, for an English final, you can study for a few hours or hundreds of hours, but the grade you get in the end will not change linearly. You will not get 400 on the final exam if you study for 400 hours (although review for that long is not necessarily possible). Some things are threshold-dependent, meaning you must pass the threshold before getting any reward. For example, for coding, you must spend hours practicing syntax and learning the quirks of the language before you can fluently write a code that may execute a fancy function.

The main issue for people is they are unaware of how much scaling matters. Some people leverage themselves extremely hard but disregard the concept of scaling. They end up being just as burned out and unproductive as people who do not manage their time. Therefore, to effectively manage your time, think about its scaling before allocating time to it.

In addition, I mentioned leveraging yourself is helpful; however, over-leveraging yourself will have consequences. You will eventually feel burned out and want to take a break. No one is immune to it, and avoiding it will make you less productive.

Another way to manage your time is to set goals as you go. Time management is mainly for self-improvement; if you do not have an end goal, time management will not be beneficial. Ideally, a goal should be explicit, critical, well-defined, measurable, relevant, achievable, and most importantly - have a due date.

While talking about setting goals, I think another concept is important. While watching a Ted Talk, I remember the speaker mentioning the term - "Paper or bust." Essentially, the idea is to write or type out your thoughts for future reference. If this step is absent, you are very likely to neglect this thought.

Coming back to goals, I mentioned that having a due date is crucial. Without a due date, you will have an excuse to defer it and ultimately neglecting it. In addition, you should have a diverse time frame in your personal agenda. The time frame of any goal should be divided into three categories - immediate, short term, and long term. Immediate goals must be finished before the day ends. These should be small, manageable tasks. Short-term goals should be achieved within a year of the assignment, like learning a language. Long-term goals should extend into the future, like a career choice.

Setting down these goals and eventually achieving them takes practice and discipline. Over time, it will get easier and eventually become your second nature.

Another key idea is there is a wide chasm between remarkable and important. Remarkable is something that we will remember, will celebrate upon. Important on the other hand could be minuscule, ignorable, but will have a huge impact on you. Habits are important; they will benefit you for life.

Habit and discipline are what motivate you. Therefore, we should build good habits. There are a few ways to build habits. We respond best to rewards. We all have cravings and desires. By setting this craving as the reward, you will very likely execute the action. We also respond well to signals. If you put a gigantic poster just above your computer screen, you will see it. In addition, never miss a habit consecutively. You will start to neglect it if this becomes a pattern. Use these techniques to build up habits effectively.

Finally, I want to summarize these tips into a condensed quote that I heard from a self-improvement speaker name Benjamin Spector - "Paper and pattern yields pleasure and productivity."
